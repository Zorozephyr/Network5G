Step 1 — Frame the exact problem statement. The novel contribution should be: a DPU-resident closed-loop AI system that observes per-slice traffic telemetry at the data plane, predicts bandwidth demand over a short horizon (50–500ms), and autonomously executes reserve/forgo actions on slice resources subject to QoS policy constraints — all within the DPU without involving the host CPU or O-RAN RIC.
Step 2 — Survey the four literature clusters separately. (a) In-NIC ML inference papers (PEARC '25, ML-NIC Frontiers 2025, the Oxford survey), (b) proactive slice provisioning papers (the DTaaS paper, ECP framework, IEEE OJCOMS 2024), (c) O-RAN dApp papers (the dApp real-time AI control paper, CollabORAN), (d) DPU offload architecture papers (BlueField-4, Pensando Pollara 400, FPGA SmartNIC shells survey Feb 2026).
Step 3 — Identify the specific AI model class. Tree-based models are what fit in P4 FPGA SmartNICs today. For a DPU with ARM cores (BlueField-4), small LSTM or temporal convolutional models are feasible. The model must run in well under 1ms. This is a real constraint to characterize early.
Step 4 — Define the "forgo" primitive formally. This is likely the most publishable novelty. What is the decision boundary for releasing reserved bandwidth? How does prediction uncertainty factor in? This is a control theory + ML problem that has no prior art in this context.
Step 5 — Build a testbed proof of concept. The NVIDIA BlueField-3 or BlueField-4 with DOCA SDK is the natural platform. Pair it with an O-RAN emulator (OpenAirInterface or srsRAN) and an NS3 or Amarisoft slice testbed to generate realistic multi-slice traffic.
Step 6 — Address the policy integration layer. Map the AI decisions to 3GPP QoS flow identifiers (5QI values) and SLA parameters. This is where the "policies" dimension your architect mentioned lives — it needs to interface with existing 5G standards, not reinvent them.
